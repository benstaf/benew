# Signals, Not Narratives

Every Demo Day produces the same cycle: a wave of breathless writeups, Twitter threads breaking down "the hottest companies," and investor lists naming their favorites. Then, six months later, half of those "hottest companies" have quietly pivoted or gone dark.

YC Bench is built on a different premise: **narratives are noise; signals are what matter**.

## What We Deliberately Ignore

Our scoring methodology explicitly excludes:

**Founder pedigree.** Whether someone went to Stanford or dropped out of a state school tells you almost nothing about their probability of building a category-defining company. The data doesn't support the myth.

**Press coverage.** Press is a lagging indicator at best. At worst, it's inversely correlated with actual progress — the companies talking loudest are often the ones shipping least.

**Investor names.** A16Z writing a check is information. It's just not *early* information. By the time an investor's name appears on a cap table, the signal has already been priced in by the market.

**Total addressable market claims.** Every deck says "the market is $50B." That's not a signal; it's a slide.

## What We Actually Track

The signals we weight are behavioral and observable:

**Shipping cadence.** Are they pushing product updates? How frequently? Are those updates driven by user feedback or internal roadmaps?

**Hiring patterns.** Who are they hiring, and for what roles? Early engineering hires suggest product bets. Early sales hires suggest they've found something worth selling.

**Community engagement.** Not follower counts — actual participation in communities where their target users live. Are they answering questions, sharing insights, showing up?

**Founder signal output.** Are they writing, speaking, or publishing about problems in their space? Founders who are intellectually engaged with their domain tend to move faster and attract better teams.

None of these is a perfect proxy for revenue or growth. But together, they form a composite picture of a team's velocity and engagement — and that picture predicts outcomes better than the narrative alternatives.

## The Hype Discount

One thing we've consistently observed: companies that generate significant Demo Day hype tend to *underperform* their cohort in the 90 days following. The causality likely runs in multiple directions — hype attracts investor meetings that consume founder time, inflated expectations make it harder to build in public, and companies with strong narratives often have weaker product foundations.

This is why YC Bench applies what we internally call the "hype discount." A company with outsized media coverage gets a lower prior in our model until their behavioral signals confirm the narrative.

## Building a Better Benchmark

The goal isn't to be contrarian for its own sake. It's to build a system that's genuinely predictive — one where a high score actually means something about future outcomes, not just present visibility.

That requires discipline: staying signal-driven even when the narrative is exciting, and staying open to narrative when the signals support it.

We think that's the right way to benchmark startups. And we're building the tools to do it at scale.
